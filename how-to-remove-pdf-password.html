How to Remove a Password from a PDF You Own

Last updated: July 2026

Bank statements, salary slips, insurance policies, and tax documents in India and many other countries are routinely emailed as password-protected PDFs — usually locked with your PAN, date of birth, or account number. The protection makes sense in transit, but it becomes a nuisance the moment you need to merge the statement with other documents, print it, or file it with a portal that rejects encrypted files. This guide covers the legitimate ways to remove a password from a PDF that belongs to you.

Important: everything below applies to documents you own or are authorised to handle. Attempting to strip protection from someone else's document without permission may be illegal in your jurisdiction and is not what these methods are for.

First, Understand the Two Kinds of PDF Passwords

  • Open password (user password). Required just to view the file. This is what banks use. Once you type it in, you can see everything — and you can also re-save an unprotected copy.
  • Permissions password (owner password). The file opens freely, but printing, copying, or editing is restricted. These restrictions are advisory and many viewers ignore them.

If you know the open password, removing it is trivial. If you have genuinely lost the password to your own document, recovery is much harder — skip to the last section.

Method 1: Print to PDF (Works Everywhere)

  1. Open the PDF and enter the password when prompted.
  2. Press Ctrl+P (Cmd+P on Mac).
  3. Choose "Save as PDF" / "Microsoft Print to PDF" as the printer.
  4. Save the new file. It is an exact visual copy with no password.

This works in Chrome, Edge, Firefox, Adobe Reader, and every mainstream viewer, on Windows, macOS, and Linux. On Android and iOS the share/print dialog offers the same "Save as PDF" option. The only downside: if the original had a selectable text layer, the printed copy sometimes converts it to an image, making the text non-searchable.

Method 2: Re-save Without Encryption

Some tools preserve the text layer perfectly rather than re-printing the pages:

  • macOS Preview: open the file, enter the password, then File → Export as PDF. Leave "Encrypt" unticked.
  • LibreOffice Draw (free, all platforms): open the PDF, then File → Export as PDF with no password set.
  • Command line (advanced users): the open-source qpdf utility removes encryption in one step: qpdf --password=YOURPASS --decrypt locked.pdf unlocked.pdf. This is the cleanest method — the file is byte-for-byte the same document minus the encryption layer.

Method 3 to Avoid: Random "Unlock PDF" Websites

Searching "unlock PDF online" returns dozens of sites that ask you to upload the file and the password. Think about what that means for a bank statement: you are handing a stranger's server your full transaction history and the password pattern your bank uses (often your PAN or date of birth — credentials that unlock every other statement you will ever receive). Even if the site is honest, files sit on their servers and in their logs. For financial and identity documents, only use methods that run on your own device.

Why Portals and Mergers Reject Encrypted PDFs

Encryption scrambles the internal structure of the file. A merging tool cannot copy pages out of a document it cannot read, and most e-filing portals refuse encrypted uploads because their systems must be able to open, stamp, and archive the file. The workflow that always works:

  1. Remove passwords from each statement or slip using Method 1 or 2.
  2. Merge the unlocked files into one PDF in the required order.
  3. Upload or email the single combined file.

Lost the Password to Your Own File?

For bank and payroll documents, don't fight the encryption — the password is almost always documented in the covering email (typically a combination like the first four letters of your name plus your birth year, or your PAN in capitals). Check the email that delivered the file, or simply re-download the statement from your bank's portal, where unprotected copies are often available. For your own archived files with a genuinely forgotten strong password, realistic recovery is limited: modern PDF encryption (AES-256) is not practically breakable, and no online service can honestly promise otherwise.
